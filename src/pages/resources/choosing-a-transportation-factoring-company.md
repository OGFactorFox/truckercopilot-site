There is no single best factoring company for trucking. The right provider for a one-truck owner operator hauling spot freight is often the wrong one for a 30-truck fleet with contract lanes, and vice versa. Anyone who tells you one company is best for everyone is usually selling that company. What actually exists is a set of evaluation criteria, and a factoring agreement that fits your operation on every one of them.

Factoring, briefly: you sell your freight invoices to a finance company at a discount, and they advance you most of the invoice value now instead of you waiting 30 to 60 days for the broker to pay. The details of that arrangement, the rate, the advance, the recourse terms, the contract length, are where good and bad deals diverge. If you want the mechanics from the ground up first, start with [the complete carrier guide to freight factoring](/resources/freight-factoring-guide), then come back to the checklist.

## The criteria that separate providers

- **Transportation experience.** Trucking receivables are their own world: rate confirmations, detention, lumpers, broker bonds, double-brokering fraud. A specialty transportation finance company that knows this terrain will verify loads faster and fight for your invoices more effectively than a generalist that also factors staffing agencies.
- **Rate and fee transparency.** The headline rate is only part of the cost. Ask about ACH fees, wire fees, invoice processing fees, credit check fees, monthly minimum shortfall fees, and reserve handling. A slightly higher flat rate with no add-ons can beat a low teaser rate wrapped in fees.
- **Recourse structure.** With recourse factoring, if the broker never pays, the invoice comes back to you. Nonrecourse shifts some credit risk to the provider, but read exactly which risks are covered, because nonrecourse rarely covers disputes or shortages, and terms differ by provider. Neither structure is automatically better; they are priced differently for a reason.
- **Contract length and exit terms.** How long is the term, does it auto-renew, what notice window do you have to cancel, and what does it cost to leave early? Also ask how UCC filings and buyout processes work if you switch providers later.
- **Minimum volume requirements.** Some agreements require you to factor a minimum dollar amount or all of your invoices. If you plan to let strong-paying customers pay direct, make sure the contract allows it.
- **The funding process itself.** What documents are required, what are the daily cutoffs, how is verification handled, and what banking rails do they fund on? The mechanics behind timing are worth understanding in detail; we walk through them in [Same Day Freight Funding: What Determines the Actual Timing](/resources/same-day-freight-funding).
- **Customer service.** When a broker disputes an invoice at 4 p.m. on a Friday, do you get a person who knows your account? Ask who your contact will be and how disputes are actually worked.
- **Technology.** Submitting documents, checking funding status, and running broker credit checks should not require faxes and phone tag. The provider's tools, and how they connect to yours, affect your daily workload more than most contract terms.

## Questions to ask before signing

- What is my all-in cost on a typical $2,000 invoice, including every fee?
- Is this recourse or nonrecourse, and exactly which events are covered?
- What is the contract term, the auto-renewal language, and the cancellation notice window?
- Are there monthly minimums, and what happens if I miss them?
- Which of my customers are credit-approved today, and how do I check a new broker before booking?
- What are your cutoff times and funding methods, and what does each cost?
- How are reserves held and released?

Get the answers in writing and read them against the contract. If the salesperson's answer and the contract disagree, the contract wins.

## Red flags

Walk carefully when you see vague or verbal-only fee explanations, long auto-renew terms with narrow cancellation windows measured in days, pressure to sign before you have read the full agreement, termination fees that are not clearly stated, or a provider that cannot clearly explain its own recourse terms. None of these prove a bad actor, but all of them are reasons to slow down and compare.
