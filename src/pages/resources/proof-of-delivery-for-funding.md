A proof of delivery for a trucking invoice is the document that shows the freight actually arrived and the receiver accepted it. In most cases it is the delivery copy of the bill of lading, signed and dated by someone at the consignee, though some receivers issue their own delivery receipt. For anyone deciding whether to advance money against your invoice, the POD is the single most important piece of paper in the file, because it converts a claim ("we hauled this load") into evidence ("here is the receiver acknowledging it arrived").

That is why finance providers treat the POD as anchor evidence. A rate confirmation proves a load was booked. An invoice proves you billed for it. Only the POD proves the work was completed, which is the moment the broker's obligation to pay actually matures. Without it, a provider is being asked to fund an unverifiable receivable, and most will not.

## What a valid POD looks like

The bar is simple but strict. A usable POD is:

- **Signed** by the receiver, ideally with a printed name alongside the signature. An initial or an unreadable scrawl with no printed name is weak.
- **Dated** with the actual delivery date. An undated signature leaves the payment clock ambiguous and invites verification calls.
- **Legible** end to end. The load or PO number, consignee name, piece count, and signature block all need to be readable in the copy that gets submitted. A pristine original in the glovebox does no good if the photo is blurry.
- **Complete.** If the delivery receipt runs multiple pages, every page counts. A missing page is treated as a missing document.

It should also be consistent with the rest of the file: the consignee and address on the POD should match the rate confirmation, and the delivery date should make sense against the pickup date. Inconsistencies are one of the classic reasons invoices stall, as covered in [why freight invoices get held](/resources/why-freight-invoices-get-held).

## Exceptions noted on the POD

Sometimes the receiver writes on the document: three cartons short, one pallet crushed, seal broken on arrival. These notations are called exceptions, and they matter enormously for funding. An exception signals that the debtor may file a claim and offset it against the invoice, so the amount actually collectible is uncertain. A finance provider seeing an exception will often hold the invoice, fund it partially, or wait for the claim to resolve.

Do not treat an exception as something to obscure. A clearly documented exception, photographed and reported to the broker the same day, tends to resolve faster and with more credibility than one the broker discovers on its own weeks later. If the freight was fine and the notation was a receiver error, getting it corrected before you leave the dock is far easier than after.

## Photograph it at the dock

The best practice is mechanical: photograph the signed POD at the receiver, before the truck moves. Lay the document flat, get even light, fill the frame, and check that the signature, date, and load number are readable in the image itself, not just in real life. Capture every page. If there is an exception, photograph that notation up close as well.

Doing this at the dock does two things. It guarantees the document cannot be lost in a truck cab for a week, and it lets the invoice be submitted the same day the load delivers, which starts the payment process at the earliest possible moment. For an [owner operator](/owner-operators) living close to the margin, those recovered days matter, a point explored further in [how owner operators can protect cash flow between loads](/resources/owner-operator-cash-flow).

## When the POD is missing

A missing POD does not always mean no payment, but it always means friction. The usual fallbacks are chasing the receiver for a copy of their receiving record, asking the broker to confirm delivery in writing, or supplying secondary evidence like tracking data or a delivery photo. Each of those takes days and depends on someone else's cooperation. Some debtors simply will not pay without the signed document, and finance providers generally will not advance against an invoice they cannot anchor. The cheapest fix for a missing POD is never losing it in the first place.
