Freight factoring is selling a delivered load's invoice to a finance company so you get paid in days instead of waiting out the broker's terms. The factor advances most of the invoice value, collects from the broker itself, and keeps a fee. That is the whole idea. Everything else in factoring, the rates, the reserves, the recourse question, the paperwork, is detail hanging off that one trade: money now, a fee, and someone else doing the waiting and the chasing.

This guide walks the entire path from delivered to funded, with a worked cost example and links to deeper answers at each fork. ## How freight factoring works, step by step

**Setup, once.** You apply with a factoring company. The factor reviews your authority, your customers, and any existing liens on your receivables, then files its own security interest and sends your brokers a notice of assignment, or NOA. The NOA tells each broker that payment on your invoices now goes to the factor. This happens once per relationship, not per load.

**Check the broker before you book.** Most factors screen broker credit and will tell you whether a load is fundable before you commit the truck. This is one of the quietest benefits of factoring: you find out a broker is a payment risk while you can still say no. It replaces the homework covered in [how to check a freight broker before accepting a load](/resources/freight-broker-credit-check) with an answer from someone whose own money is on the line.

**Haul and capture the evidence.** The load moves. What determines your funding speed now is paperwork discipline: the rate confirmation, the bill of lading signed at pickup, the proof of delivery signed at the receiver, and receipts for any lumper or detention charges. The full list, and what each document must show, is in [what documents do you need to get paid for a load](/resources/documents-needed-for-freight-factoring).

**Submit the completed load.** Invoice plus documents go to the factor. Carriers using Trucker Copilot capture the documents in the [BOL scanner](/bol-scanner-app) from the cab and submit the completed load from the phone, which is exactly the step where loads used to die in truck stop scanner queues.

**Verification.** The factor confirms the load happened as billed: documents legible, totals matching the rate con, delivery confirmed, no dispute brewing. Clean submissions pass fast. Gaps generate phone calls and days of delay, which is why [why freight invoices get held](/resources/why-freight-invoices-get-held) is worth reading before your first submission, not after.

**The advance.** The factor pays you most of the invoice value, commonly the large majority of it, with the remainder held in reserve. Timing depends on the provider, your setup, and when you submit; the honest version of the "same day" promise is covered in [same day freight funding: what determines the actual timing](/resources/same-day-freight-funding).

**Collection and reserve release.** The factor collects the full invoice from the broker on the broker's timeline, then releases your reserve, minus the fee. You already had most of the money for weeks by the time this happens.

## What factoring costs

Factoring pricing has three moving parts: the fee, the advance rate, and everything in the fine print.

**The fee** is a percentage of the invoice. Some providers charge one flat rate per invoice. Others tier the rate by how long the broker takes to pay, or by your monthly volume. Advertised rates on major providers' marketing pages, as of the July 2026 research for this guide, ran from around one and a half percent to just over three percent per invoice, and your actual quote depends on your volume, your freight, your customers, and the recourse structure. Treat every number in a provider's ad as the start of the conversation, not the end.

**The advance rate** is how much you get up front, with the rest held in reserve until the broker pays. A high advance with a high fee can net you less than a lower advance with a lower fee, so run the arithmetic on both numbers together.

**A worked example, with illustrative numbers.** Say a load pays 2,500 dollars, your provider's fee is 3 percent flat, and the advance rate is 95 percent. On funding day you receive 2,375 dollars. The factor invoices the broker 2,500 dollars and, once the broker pays, releases your 125 dollar reserve minus the 75 dollar fee. You end up with 2,425 dollars, and you had 95 percent of your money within days of delivery instead of financing the broker's payment terms yourself. Whether that 75 dollars was worth it depends on what waiting costs you: fuel on a card, a truck payment, or a load you could not book because your cash was parked in someone's payables department.

**The fine print** is where comparable headline rates stop being comparable. Ask every provider the same list: minimum volume commitments, term length and what it costs to leave, ACH versus wire fees, invoice processing or funding fees, fuel advance charges, and what happens to your reserve if a broker disputes or short pays. A clean contract with a slightly higher rate routinely beats a teaser rate wrapped in fees.

## Recourse vs nonrecourse, in one minute

If the broker never pays, someone eats the loss. Recourse factoring means that someone is you: the factor charges the invoice back. Nonrecourse means the factor absorbs qualifying losses, typically for a higher fee, and the word qualifying carries weight, because coverage is usually limited to specific credit events. Neither structure is automatically better; they price different risks. The full breakdown is in [recourse and nonrecourse factoring explained for carriers](/resources/recourse-vs-nonrecourse-factoring).

## What you need to qualify

Factors underwrite your customers more than they underwrite you, because the broker is who they collect from. That is why factoring is unusually open to new authorities: a carrier with an MC number that activated last month but hauls for creditworthy brokers is often an easier approval than an established carrier hauling for shaky ones. Expect a factor to look at your authority and insurance, confirm your receivables are not already pledged to another lender, and check that your invoices are clean of disputes. Expect more scrutiny if your freight involves heavy detention fights, frequent short pays, or brokers with thin credit.

## Factoring vs quick pay, in one minute

A broker's quick pay program discounts one invoice from one broker for faster payment from that same broker. Factoring covers every approved broker through one relationship, adds credit screening and collections, and requires an actual agreement. Quick pay is a situational tool; factoring is a payment system. The head-to-head, including the notice-of-assignment conflict that catches carriers who try to run both carelessly, is in [factoring vs quick pay: which gets owner-operators paid better](/resources/factoring-vs-quick-pay).

## How to choose a factoring company

The rate matters less than the fit. The questions that separate providers: Do they know transportation, meaning freight documents, broker behavior, and detention reality, or are they a generalist receivables shop? Is the structure recourse or nonrecourse, and at what price difference? What are the contract term, the exit terms, and the minimums? How do they handle a short pay or a dispute, and how fast do they answer the phone when a funding is stuck? Can they receive your submission electronically with status you can see, or does every load disappear into an email inbox? ## Questions carriers ask

### Is factoring worth it for a one-truck operation?

Run the number against your real alternative. If waiting 30 to 45 days on brokers means borrowing, skipping loads, or floating fuel on cards, the fee is buying you working capital and collections labor. If you have months of operating cash in the bank and patient customers, you are paying for speed you may not need. The math changes as you grow; revisit it.

### Can I leave a factoring company?

Yes, subject to your contract's term and notice provisions, and the handoff has to be managed: the old factor releases its lien and the brokers get updated payment instructions. Ask about exit terms before you sign, not when you want out. Overlapping assignments freeze payments faster than almost any other paperwork problem.

### Does factoring hurt my credit?

Factoring is a sale of receivables, not a loan, and the factor's underwriting leans on your customers' credit. The factor files a lien on your receivables, which other lenders will see, and any personal guarantees or credit checks vary by provider. Ask each provider what it files and what it checks.

### What happens if the broker never pays?

Under recourse, the invoice is charged back to you. Under nonrecourse, qualifying credit losses are the factor's problem. Under both, prevention beats remedies, which is why the broker check before you book the load is the most valuable step in the entire process.
